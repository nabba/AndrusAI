# Estonian Deforestation Policy Research

## Key Concepts

**Forest Coverage and Status**
- Estonia has approximately 50% of its territory covered by forest (around 2 million hectares), making it one of the most forested countries in Europe
- In 2020, Estonia had 1.9 million hectares of natural forest, extending over 41% of its land area
- The country lost 29,000 hectares of natural forest in recent years, equivalent to 10 Mt of CO₂ emissions
- Estonia is the EU's second-largest exporter of wood pellets after Latvia

**Legal Framework**
- Estonian Forest Law (adopted 1993, with new versions prepared based on Estonian Forest Policy of 1997)
- Law on the Protection of Nature (1990) and Law of Sustainable Development (1995) serve as frame laws
- Forest Act regulates felling permits, forest management, and deforestation rules
- Natura 2000 network designates protected areas under EU Habitats and Birds Directives

**Key Policy Drivers of Deforestation**
- EU Renewable Energy Directive allows member states to subsidize burning woody biomass as "green energy"
- Estonia receives 94% of its renewable energy from biomass
- Logging intensity in 2016-2018 was 85% higher than in 2004-2015
- Logging volumes have almost tripled in the past decade
- At least half of logged wood is used for bioenergy

**Protected Areas Under Pressure**
- In the last 10 years, the Estonian government issued logging permits for 82,000 hectares of Natura 2000 protected habitat forests (equivalent to 115,000 football fields)
- Natura 2000 forest areas are divided into zones with different protection levels
- Clear-cutting is typically limited to max 1 hectare in Natura 2000 areas
- Supreme Court has ruled that logging in internationally protected forests is no longer permissible

**Carbon Sink Collapse**
- Estonia's forests were expected to become net carbon emitters by 2034, but became net sources as early as 2020
- Preliminary data shows Estonia's forests have shifted from carbon sinks to carbon sources
- This threatens climate neutrality targets

**Institutional Structure**
- Ministry of Climate (formerly Ministry of Environment) oversees forestry
- State Forest Management Center (RMK) manages state forests
- Environment Agency collects and analyzes forestry data
- County Forest Departments control private forestry

## Best Practices

**Policy Recommendations from Research**
1. **Reduce Felling Volumes**: Studies show cutting felling volumes produces immediate climate and biodiversity benefits. A reduction of 1 million cubic meters creates a positive sink effect of -1.5 Mt CO₂

2. **Strengthen Protected Area Enforcement**: The Supreme Court ruling should be fully implemented—no logging in internationally protected forests

3. **Reform EU Biomass Subsidies**: Stop counting wood taken directly from forests toward renewable energy targets; redirect subsidies to truly sustainable alternatives

4. **Conduct Proper Impact Assessments**: Require Habitat Directive-mandated impact assessments before any logging in protected areas

5. **Continuous-Cover Forestry**: Implement on organic soils to reduce emissions from peatland disturbance

6. **Increase Conservation Areas**: Expand strictly protected zones where no commercial activity is permitted

7. **Leave More Dead Wood**: Maintain forest ecosystem health by leaving dead wood in forests rather than clearing it

8. **Address Illegal Logging**: Strengthen state control over natural resources; in 1997, over 1,100 violations were registered, with illegal logging causing losses of over 50,000 m³ annually

9. **Transparent Permit System**: Annull unused logging permits and halt issuance of new permits in protected areas

10. **Public Awareness Campaigns**: Research indicates low Estonian public interest in environmental issues; education and engagement are critical
