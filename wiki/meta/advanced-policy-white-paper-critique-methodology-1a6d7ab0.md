# Advanced Policy White Paper Critique Methodology

## Key Concepts

### The READ Approach
A systematic four-step framework for rigorous document analysis in policy research:
- **Ready your materials**: Establish parameters for document selection including topic scope, date ranges, and search locations. Determine sample size based on research questions and time allocation.
- **Extract data**: Systematically collect relevant information using structured coding tools. Skim documents first for overview, then read to identify categories of analysis.
- **Analyse data**: Apply qualitative or quantitative methods to interpret extracted data. Recognize documents as "social facts" created and consumed within social contexts.
- **Distil your findings**: Synthesize insights into coherent conclusions that address research questions.

### CDC Policy Analytical Framework
A structured approach for identifying, analyzing, and prioritizing policies:
- **Domain 1 - Problem Identification**: Define the problem specifically (burden, frequency, severity, scope). Frame problems in ways that illuminate policy solutions.
- **Domain 2 - Policy Analysis**: Identify policy options through literature review, best practice surveys, and environmental scans. Assess options against three interrelated criteria: health impact, feasibility, and economic/budgetary impacts.
- **Domain 3 - Strategy and Policy Development**: Clarify operational issues, identify and educate stakeholders, and conduct additional analyses to support adoption and implementation.

### TARP Source Evaluation Framework
Four-factor criteria for evaluating white paper sources:
- **Timeliness**: How recent is the source? In fast-moving fields, sources older than 5 years may lack relevance. Consider pre- and post-pandemic contexts.
- **Authority**: What weight or gravitas does the source carry with the target audience? Consider trade publications, associations, and recognized experts.
- **Relevance**: How on-target is the source? Evaluate congruent logic (does it match your argument?), parallel SEO (keyword alignment), and presence of punchy soundbites.
- **Proximity**: How close does the source feel to the reader? Consider shared industry, job role, geography, or common challenges.

### Critical Assessment Checklist
Ten criteria for evaluating policy publications:
1. Relevance of study question
2. Contribution of new information to existing literature
3. Type of research question being addressed
4. Appropriateness of study design
5. Bias appraisal and mitigation
6. Adherence to study protocol
7. Hypothesis testing rigor
8. Statistical analysis validity
9. Conclusion validation against evidence
10. Identification of conflicts of interest

### White Paper Structural Analysis
Key components to evaluate in policy white papers:
- **Title**: Descriptive and specific to the policy issue
- **Executive Summary**: Concise overview of problem, solutions, and recommendations
- **Introduction/Background**: Context including historical, legal, technological, and stakeholder elements
- **Problem Description**: Core problem, causes, and impacts with supporting evidence
- **Criteria for Acceptable Solutions**: Standards that any viable solution must meet
- **Solution Section**: Detailed presentation of options with evidence and logical alignment to problem causes
- **Evaluation/Critique**: Strengths and weaknesses of proposed solutions
- **Recommendations**: Actionable plans with feasibility and effectiveness evidence
- **Conclusion**: Restatement of key points or case for action
- **References**: Quality and credibility of cited sources

## Best Practices

### Before Critiquing: Establish Context
- Identify the target audience (legislators, executives, citizens, technical experts)
- Determine the white paper's purpose (inform decision-making, advocate for specific solution, market a product)
- Assess the author's expertise and potential biases
- Understand the policy landscape and existing literature on the topic

### Triangulation and Cross-Validation
- Combine document analysis with interviews and observation to verify claims
- Cross-reference sources within documents and across multiple documents
- Use document analysis to generate questions for stakeholder interviews
- Alternate between documents and interviews as information sources

### Recognize Documents as Social Artifacts
- Understand that documents are created within "fields of action" and reflect power relations
- Consider motivated authorship and institutional interests
- Identify gaps in available information as potential indicators of suppressed or marginalized perspectives
- Analyze how information is framed and presented formally

### Evaluate Problem-Solution Alignment
- Verify that the problem description includes core problem, causes, and impacts
- Check that proposed solutions address identified causes, not just symptoms
- Assess whether criteria for acceptable solutions are clearly defined and applied consistently
- Examine whether multiple solutions are considered or if the paper advocates prematurely for one option

### Assess Evidence Quality
- Distinguish between primary sources (first-hand accounts, original data) and secondary sources
- Prioritize evidence from systematic reviews, peer-reviewed research, and official statistics
- Evaluate sample sizes, methodology transparency, and reproducibility
- Check for recency of data and relevance to the current policy context

### Document the Evaluation Process
- Systematically record evidence, data, and reasoning used to assign ratings
- Use structured matrices or coding tools to ensure consistency across evaluations
- Note concerns about data quality or quantity for each criterion
- Maintain transparency about subjective judgments in the assessment process

### Identify Gaps and Limitations
- Assess whether "no policy change" was considered as an option
- Evaluate if alternative or opposing viewpoints are addressed
- Determine if the white paper identifies areas where evidence is insufficient
- Consider whether recommendations for further research or incremental approaches are included
